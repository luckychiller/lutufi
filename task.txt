**What still needs strengthening**

The DBN section is too thin relative to its importance. You have the transition model and observation model stated correctly but you are missing the inference algorithms that make DBNs useful — specifically the Forward Algorithm and the Kalman Filter as a special continuous case. More importantly you should mention Hidden Markov Models as a special case of DBNs since many of your target users will already know HMMs and that connection gives them an immediate bridge into your framework. Also worth stating explicitly — how does lutufi handle the case where the transition structure itself changes over time, not just the state values? That is where real social networks live and your document currently implies the transition model is fixed.

The causal modeling section mentions SCMs and the do-operator correctly but understates what this actually requires from the user. The do-operator is only valid if the graph is correctly specified as a causal graph — not just a statistical dependency graph. These are different things and conflating them is one of the most common and consequential mistakes in applied causal inference. A sentence or two making this distinction explicit would show genuine depth.

The identifiability section is correct but thin. You should mention the ID algorithm — Shpitser and Pearl's algorithm for determining identifiability from graph structure — because this is the practical tool your users will need. Also mention that in the presence of hidden confounders identifiability often fails and what that means practically for a researcher who wants to use lutufi's causal tools.

The computational complexity section correctly states NP-hardness and treewidth but does not give the reader any intuition for what treewidth means practically. A sentence like "a network with treewidth below 20 is generally tractable for exact inference while social networks with dense community structure often have treewidth in the hundreds" would ground the abstraction in something actionable.

**What is still missing**

Variational inference deserves a mention alongside LBP and MCMC. Mean field variational inference in particular is widely used for large-scale probabilistic models and the fact that you mention LBP and MCMC but not variational methods will be noticed by machine learning researchers who are increasingly important in your target audience.

Parameter learning is completely absent. Your document covers inference — reasoning about unknown variables given known parameters — but says nothing about learning the parameters themselves from data. Maximum likelihood estimation, Bayesian parameter estimation, and the EM algorithm's role in learning versus its role in handling missing data should have at least one section. Researchers will immediately ask how lutufi learns its probability tables from real network data.

Structure learning is also absent — the problem of inferring the graph structure itself from data rather than having it specified by the user. This is arguably harder than parameter learning and more important for real research applications where the true causal graph is unknown. Even a paragraph acknowledging this as an open challenge or planned feature would show awareness.

The relationship between MRFs and BNs could be stated more precisely. You mention that lutufi treats cyclic networks as MRFs or factor graphs but you do not explain the formal relationship — that every BN can be converted to an MRF by moralizing the graph, and that this conversion loses some independence information. A specialist will want to know whether lutufi reasons on the original DAG or the moralized graph when handling mixed structures.
