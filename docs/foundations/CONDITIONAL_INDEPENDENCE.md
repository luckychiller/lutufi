## 1. Introduction

Conditional independence is the mathematical concept that makes probabilistic reasoning over large systems tractable. Without it, representing the joint probability distribution over $n$ variables requires specifying an exponential number of parameters — $O(k^n)$ for variables with $k$ states. With conditional independence, this distribution can be factored into a product of smaller, local distributions, each involving only a handful of variables. The entire edifice of probabilistic graphical models — Bayesian networks, Markov random fields, factor graphs, and their extensions — rests on encoding, exploiting, and reasoning about conditional independence relationships.

This document provides a comprehensive treatment of conditional independence: its formal mathematical definition, its algebraic properties, its representation in graphical models, the algorithms for determining it from graph structure, the statistical methods for testing it from data, its peculiar challenges in social network contexts, and its implementation in the Lutufi library. It is written as a standalone deep-dive reference intended for researchers, developers, and domain experts who need a thorough understanding of this foundational concept.

The importance of conditional independence for Lutufi cannot be overstated. Lutufi unifies Bayesian networks with social and economic network analysis, and the core of this unification is the claim that network structure encodes probabilistic independence. When person $A$ is connected to person $B$, who is connected to person $C$, but $A$ is not connected to $C$, the network structure asserts something about independence: knowing $B$'s state may render $A$ and $C$ conditionally independent. Whether this assertion holds — and when it fails — determines whether the model's inferences are trustworthy.

## 2. Formal Definition of Conditional Independence

### 2.1 The Basic Definition

Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ be a probability space, and let $X$, $Y$, and $Z$ be random variables (or vectors of random variables) defined on this space. We say that $X$ is **conditionally independent** of $Y$ given $Z$, and write:

$$
X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z
$$

if and only if:

$$
P(X \leq x, Y \leq y \mid Z = z) = P(X \leq x \mid Z = z) \cdot P(Y \leq y \mid Z = z)
$$

for all values $x$, $y$, and for all $z$ such that $P(Z = z) > 0$.

This definition says that once we know the value of $Z$, learning the value of $Y$ provides no additional information about $X$ (and vice versa). The conditional distribution of $X$ given $Z$ is the same whether or not we also know $Y$.

### 2.2 Equivalent Definitions

The definition above can be expressed in several equivalent ways, each illuminating a different facet of the concept:

**Definition via conditional densities.** If $X$, $Y$, and $Z$ have a joint density $f(x, y, z)$, then $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$ if and only if:

$$
f(x, y \mid z) = f(x \mid z) \cdot f(y \mid z)
$$

for all $x$, $y$, $z$ with $f(z) > 0$. Equivalently, $f(x \mid y, z) = f(x \mid z)$ whenever $f(y, z) > 0$.

**Definition via sigma-algebras (Dawid, 1979).** In the measure-theoretic formulation, let $\sigma(X)$, $\sigma(Y)$, $\sigma(Z)$ denote the sigma-algebras generated by $X$, $Y$, $Z$ respectively. Then $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$ if and only if for every bounded measurable function $g$ of $X$:

$$
E[g(X) \mid \sigma(Y), \sigma(Z)] = E[g(X) \mid \sigma(Z)] \quad \text{a.s.}
$$

This formulation is the most general — it does not require densities or even countability.

**Definition via factorization.** $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$ if and only if the conditional joint distribution factors as:

$$
P(X, Y \mid Z) = h(X, Z) \cdot g(Y, Z)
$$

for some non-negative functions $h$ and $g$. This is a consequence of the Hammersley-Clifford factorization principle.

**Definition via information theory.** $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$ if and only if the conditional mutual information is zero:

$$
I(X; Y \mid Z) = 0
$$

where $I(X; Y \mid Z) = \sum_{x, y, z} P(x, y, z) \log \frac{P(x, y \mid z)}{P(x \mid z) P(y \mid z)}$ for discrete variables, or the analogous integral for continuous ones.

### 2.3 Marginal (Unconditional) Independence

The special case where the conditioning set is empty gives **marginal independence** (also called unconditional independence):

$$
X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y
$$

meaning $P(X, Y) = P(X) \cdot P(Y)$. Marginal independence is a strictly stronger condition than conditional independence for some conditioning set; that is, $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y$ does not imply $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$, and $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$ does not imply $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y$.

**Example.** Let $X$ and $Y$ be independent fair coin flips, and let $Z = X \oplus Y$ (the XOR). Then $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y$ (marginally independent), but $X \not\!\perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$ (conditionally dependent given $Z$). Conversely, consider a collider structure $X \to Z \leftarrow Y$ in a Bayesian network: $X$ and $Y$ may be conditionally independent given nothing ($X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y$), but become dependent given $Z$ ($X \not\!\perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$).

### 2.4 Notation Conventions

Several notation conventions exist in the literature:

- $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$ — The double perpendicular symbol (Dawid, 1979). This is the standard in statistics and graphical models.
- $(X \perp Y \mid Z)_P$ — Indicates the CI holds in distribution $P$. Used when multiple distributions are under discussion.
- $(X \perp Y \mid Z)_{\mathcal{G}}$ — Indicates the CI is asserted by graph $\mathcal{G}$ (via d-separation or separation).
- $I(X, Z, Y)$ or $\langle X, Z \mid Y \rangle$ — Older notations from the expert systems literature.

Throughout Lutufi's documentation, we use the $\perp\!\!\!\perp$ notation and write $X \perp_{\mathcal{G}} Y \mid Z$ when the assertion comes from a graph.

### 2.5 Conditional Independence for Sets of Variables

The definition extends naturally to sets. Let $\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{C}$ be disjoint sets of random variables. Then:

$$
\mathbf{A} \perp\!\!\!\perp \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C}
$$

means that, given the values of all variables in $\mathbf{C}$, the variables in $\mathbf{A}$ are jointly independent of the variables in $\mathbf{B}$. This set-based formulation is essential for graphical models, where d-separation and graph separation operate on sets of nodes.

## 3. Properties of Conditional Independence

Conditional independence satisfies a set of algebraic properties known as the **semi-graphoid axioms** (Pearl, 1988; Pearl and Paz, 1987). These properties constrain the set of CI relationships that can hold simultaneously in any probability distribution. Understanding them is essential for structure learning, CI testing, and reasoning about model implications.

### 3.1 Symmetry

$$
X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad Y \perp\!\!\!\perp X \mid Z
$$

If $X$ is conditionally independent of $Y$ given $Z$, then $Y$ is conditionally independent of $X$ given $Z$. This follows immediately from the symmetry of the factorization $P(X, Y \mid Z) = P(X \mid Z) P(Y \mid Z)$.

**Significance:** Symmetry means that conditional independence is a symmetric binary relation (given the conditioning set). This symmetry is reflected in undirected graphical models, where an absent edge between $X$ and $Y$ encodes a symmetric independence claim.

### 3.2 Decomposition

$$
X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y, W \mid Z \quad \Longrightarrow \quad X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z
$$

If $X$ is conditionally independent of the pair $(Y, W)$ given $Z$, then $X$ is conditionally independent of $Y$ alone given $Z$. Knowing $Y$ and $W$ together tells you nothing about $X$ beyond $Z$, so knowing $Y$ alone also tells you nothing.

**Significance:** Decomposition allows us to derive simpler CI statements from more complex ones. If a large set of variables is independent of $X$ given $Z$, then every subset is also independent of $X$ given $Z$.

### 3.3 Weak Union

$$
X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y, W \mid Z \quad \Longrightarrow \quad X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z, W
$$

If $X$ is conditionally independent of $(Y, W)$ given $Z$, then conditioning additionally on $W$ preserves the independence of $X$ and $Y$. Intuitively: if the combined knowledge of $Y$ and $W$ is irrelevant to $X$ (given $Z$), then learning $W$ cannot make $Y$ suddenly relevant.

**Significance:** Weak union is crucial for the soundness of graphical model reasoning. It ensures that adding variables to the conditioning set does not create spurious dependencies.

### 3.4 Contraction

$$
X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z, W \quad \text{and} \quad X \perp\!\!\!\perp W \mid Z \quad \Longrightarrow \quad X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y, W \mid Z
$$

If $X$ is independent of $Y$ given $(Z, W)$, and independently $X$ is independent of $W$ given $Z$ alone, then $X$ is independent of the pair $(Y, W)$ given $Z$. Contraction is the "reverse" of decomposition: it allows combining two CI statements into a stronger one.

**Significance:** Contraction enables building up complex CI statements from simpler ones, which is the logical basis of constraint-based structure learning algorithms like the PC algorithm.

### 3.5 Intersection (For Strictly Positive Distributions)

$$
X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z, W \quad \text{and} \quad X \perp\!\!\!\perp W \mid Z, Y \quad \Longrightarrow \quad X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y, W \mid Z
$$

If $X$ is independent of $Y$ given $(Z, W)$ and also independent of $W$ given $(Z, Y)$, then $X$ is independent of $(Y, W)$ given $Z$. This property holds only for **strictly positive distributions** — distributions where $P(\mathbf{x}) > 0$ for all configurations $\mathbf{x}$.

**Why positivity is required:** When probabilities can be exactly zero, conditioning can create degenerate distributions where the intersection property fails. Consider a distribution where certain configurations are impossible; conditioning on them creates undefined or degenerate probabilities that can violate the intersection axiom.

**Historical note:** Distributions satisfying all five properties (symmetry, decomposition, weak union, contraction, intersection) are called **graphoids** (Pearl and Paz, 1987). Distributions satisfying only the first four are called **semi-graphoids**. The distinction matters because:
- All probability distributions are semi-graphoids.
- Only strictly positive distributions are graphoids.
- Undirected graphical models (MRFs) encode the CI structure of graphoids — their separation semantics correspond exactly to the graphoid axioms.
- Directed graphical models (BNs) encode a broader class of CI structures via d-separation.

### 3.6 Composition (Does NOT Hold in General)

$$
X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z \quad \text{and} \quad X \perp\!\!\!\perp W \mid Z \quad \not\!\!\!\Longrightarrow \quad X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y, W \mid Z
$$

Composition — the claim that two separate independencies combine into a joint one — does **not** hold in general. This is a common source of errors. Knowing that $X$ is independent of $Y$ (given $Z$) and separately independent of $W$ (given $Z$) does not mean $X$ is independent of $(Y, W)$ jointly (given $Z$). The pair $(Y, W)$ can carry information about $X$ even when neither alone does.

**Example.** Let $X = Y \oplus W$ (exclusive or) with $Y$ and $W$ independent uniform bits. Then $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y$ and $X \perp\!\!\!\perp W$, but $X$ is completely determined by $(Y, W)$, so $X \not\!\perp\!\!\!\perp (Y, W)$.

**Significance for Lutufi:** The failure of composition means that Lutufi cannot assume that pairwise conditional independencies imply joint conditional independence. This has implications for both structure learning and inference.

### 3.7 Summary Table of Properties

| Property | Statement | Holds for |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Symmetry | $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z \Leftrightarrow Y \perp\!\!\!\perp X \mid Z$ | All distributions |
| Decomposition | $X \perp\!\!\!\perp YW \mid Z \Rightarrow X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$ | All distributions |
| Weak Union | $X \perp\!\!\!\perp YW \mid Z \Rightarrow X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid ZW$ | All distributions |
| Contraction | $(X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid ZW) \wedge (X \perp\!\!\!\perp W \mid Z) \Rightarrow X \perp\!\!\!\perp YW \mid Z$ | All distributions |
| Intersection | $(X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid ZW) \wedge (X \perp\!\!\!\perp W \mid ZY) \Rightarrow X \perp\!\!\!\perp YW \mid Z$ | Strictly positive distributions |
| Composition | $(X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z) \wedge (X \perp\!\!\!\perp W \mid Z) \Rightarrow X \perp\!\!\!\perp YW \mid Z$ | **Not generally valid** |

## 4. Graphical Representations of Conditional Independence

The central insight of probabilistic graphical models is that conditional independence relationships can be compactly represented by a graph, and that algorithms can read off these relationships directly from the graph structure without computing any probabilities.

### 4.1 Three Types of Graphs

**Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs)** encode CI through **d-separation** — a graph-theoretic criterion involving three types of path structures (chains, forks, colliders). DAGs are the graph structure underlying Bayesian networks and structural causal models.

**Undirected Graphs** encode CI through **graph separation** — the simple criterion that removing the conditioning set disconnects the graph between the query variables. Undirected graphs underlie Markov random fields.

**Factor Graphs** are bipartite graphs with variable nodes and factor nodes. CI in factor graphs is determined by graph separation in the underlying structure, treating factor nodes as intermediaries. Factor graphs are Lutufi's canonical internal representation because they subsume both DAGs and undirected graphs.

### 4.2 The Markov Properties Link Graphs to Distributions

For each type of graph, there is a formal statement — a **Markov property** — that specifies precisely how the graph encodes conditional independence:

**For DAGs (Bayesian networks):**
- *Local Markov Property:* Each variable is conditionally independent of its non-descendants given its parents.
- *Global Markov Property:* D-separation in the graph implies conditional independence in the distribution.
- *Factorization Property:* The joint distribution factors as $P(\mathbf{X}) = \prod_i P(X_i \mid \text{Pa}(X_i))$.

These three properties are equivalent for any probability distribution (Verma and Pearl, 1988; Lauritzen et al., 1990).

**For undirected graphs (MRFs):**
- *Pairwise Markov Property:* Non-adjacent variables are conditionally independent given all other variables.
- *Local Markov Property:* Each variable is conditionally independent of all others given its neighbors (Markov blanket).
- *Global Markov Property:* Separation in the graph implies conditional independence.
- *Factorization Property:* The joint distribution factors over cliques.

These properties are equivalent for strictly positive distributions (Hammersley-Clifford theorem, 1971), but not in general.

**For factor graphs:** The Markov properties follow from the structure of the bipartite graph. Two variable nodes are conditionally independent given a set of other variable nodes if every path between them in the factor graph passes through a variable node in the conditioning set. This unifies the DAG and undirected notions when the factor graph is derived from either model type.

### 4.3 Why Multiple Representations Matter

Different graph types encode different CI structures. Consider three variables $X$, $Y$, $Z$ with the following CI:
- $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y$ (marginally independent)
- $X \not\!\perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$ (conditionally dependent given $Z$)

This is the **collider** or **explaining away** pattern. A DAG can represent this perfectly: $X \to Z \leftarrow Y$. An undirected graph **cannot** represent it — in an undirected graph, marginal independence requires no path between $X$ and $Y$, but conditional dependence given $Z$ requires a path through $Z$, which is a contradiction.

Conversely, consider:
- $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$
- $X \not\!\perp\!\!\!\perp Y$

where $Z$ is a common cause: $X \leftarrow Z \to Y$. Both DAGs and undirected graphs can represent this.

These representational differences are why Lutufi maintains a multi-representation architecture. The choice of graph type determines which CI structures the model can express, and choosing the wrong representation leads to either encoding false independencies or failing to encode true ones.

## 5. D-Separation in Detail

D-separation (directed separation) is the graphical criterion for reading conditional independence from directed acyclic graphs. It was introduced by Pearl (1986, 1988) and formalized by Geiger, Verma, and Pearl (1990). D-separation is the foundation of all conditional independence reasoning in Bayesian networks and causal models.

### 5.1 Formal Definition

Let $\mathcal{G}$ be a DAG over variables $\mathbf{V}$, and let $\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{C}$ be three disjoint subsets of $\mathbf{V}$.

**Definition.** $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ are **d-separated** by $\mathbf{C}$ in $\mathcal{G}$, denoted $\mathbf{A} \perp_{\mathcal{G}} \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C}$, if for every undirected path $\pi$ between any node $a \in \mathbf{A}$ and any node $b \in \mathbf{B}$, the path $\pi$ is **blocked** by $\mathbf{C}$.

A path $\pi$ is **blocked** by $\mathbf{C}$ if there exists a node $W$ on $\pi$ such that one of the following conditions holds:

1. **$W$ is a non-collider on $\pi$ (chain or fork) and $W \in \mathbf{C}$:** The path passes through $W$ as $\cdots \to W \to \cdots$ (chain) or $\cdots \leftarrow W \to \cdots$ (fork), and $W$ is observed.

2. **$W$ is a collider on $\pi$ and neither $W$ nor any descendant of $W$ is in $\mathbf{C}$:** The path passes through $W$ as $\cdots \to W \leftarrow \cdots$ (collider), and $W$ and all its descendants are unobserved.

A path is **active** (or **d-connecting**) given $\mathbf{C}$ if it is not blocked — every non-collider on the path is outside $\mathbf{C}$, and every collider on the path has either itself or a descendant in $\mathbf{C}$.

### 5.2 The Three Structural Patterns

D-separation is built from three elementary structures. Understanding these is essential for all graphical model reasoning.

#### 5.2.1 Chains (Causal Chains / Mediation)

Structure: $X \to W \to Y$

- **$W$ not observed ($W \notin \mathbf{C}$):** Path is active. $X$ and $Y$ are d-connected. Information flows from $X$ through $W$ to $Y$.
- **$W$ observed ($W \in \mathbf{C}$):** Path is blocked. $X \perp_{\mathcal{G}} Y \mid W$. Conditioning on the mediator blocks information flow.

*Social network example:* Consider information propagation in a network. Person $X$ shares news with Person $W$, who then shares it with Person $Y$. If we know exactly what $W$ heard and told $Y$, then knowing what $X$ originally said provides no additional information about $Y$'s beliefs. The mediator screens off the source from the recipient.

#### 5.2.2 Forks (Common Causes / Confounders)

Structure: $X \leftarrow W \to Y$

- **$W$ not observed ($W \notin \mathbf{C}$):** Path is active. $X$ and $Y$ are d-connected. Both are influenced by the common cause $W$, creating a spurious correlation.
- **$W$ observed ($W \in \mathbf{C}$):** Path is blocked. $X \perp_{\mathcal{G}} Y \mid W$. Conditioning on the common cause removes the spurious association.

*Social network example:* Consider socioeconomic status ($W$) as a common cause of both network centrality ($X$) and health outcomes ($Y$). Wealthy individuals tend to be both more central in professional networks and healthier. Without controlling for socioeconomic status, centrality appears to predict health — a confounded association. Controlling for $W$ removes this spurious correlation, revealing the true (potentially much weaker) relationship.

#### 5.2.3 Colliders (Common Effects / Selection / Explaining Away)

Structure: $X \to W \leftarrow Y$

- **$W$ not observed and no descendant of $W$ in $\mathbf{C}$:** Path is blocked. $X \perp_{\mathcal{G}} Y$. The two causes are marginally independent.
- **$W$ observed ($W \in \mathbf{C}$) or any descendant of $W$ is in $\mathbf{C}$:** Path is active. $X \not\!\perp_{\mathcal{G}} Y \mid W$. Conditioning on a common effect (or its descendant) creates a spurious dependence between its causes.

*Social network example (Berkson's paradox):* Consider a prestigious social club ($W$) that selects members based on either high academic achievement ($X$) or strong social connections ($Y$). Among the general population, academic achievement and social connections may be independent. But among club members (conditioning on $W$), they become negatively correlated: members who are not particularly academic must have gotten in through social connections, and vice versa. This selection bias is a direct consequence of conditioning on a collider.

*Social network example (the explaining away effect):* A rumor ($W$) at a company might originate from either a leak from the executive team ($X$) or a lucky guess by an analyst ($Y$). If we observe the rumor but don't know its source, learning that an executive leaked confirms the source and "explains away" the analyst — making the analyst guess seem less likely. The two potential causes interact through the observed effect.

### 5.3 D-Separation Algorithm

The following algorithm determines whether $\mathbf{A} \perp_{\mathcal{G}} \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C}$:

**Input:** DAG $\mathcal{G}$, disjoint sets $\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{C}$.

**Step 1 — Ancestral set.** Compute $\mathbf{D} = \text{An}(\mathbf{A} \cup \mathbf{B} \cup \mathbf{C})$, the set of all ancestors of the query and conditioning nodes, plus the nodes themselves. Remove all other nodes and their edges.

**Step 2 — Moralize.** In the ancestral subgraph, add undirected edges between all pairs of nodes that share a common child (marry the parents). Drop all edge directions.

**Step 3 — Separate.** Remove the nodes in $\mathbf{C}$ (and all their edges) from the moralized ancestral graph. If $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ are disconnected in the resulting graph, then $\mathbf{A} \perp_{\mathcal{G}} \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C}$.

**Correctness:** This algorithm is equivalent to the path-based definition of d-separation (Lauritzen et al., 1990).

**Complexity:** $O(|\mathbf{V}| + |\mathbf{E}|)$ — linear in the graph size. This is achieved by graph traversal (BFS or DFS) on the moralized ancestral graph.

### 5.4 The Bayes Ball Algorithm

The **Bayes Ball algorithm** (Shachter, 1998) provides an alternative, more intuitive procedure for determining d-separation. The metaphor is a ball rolling through the graph, with the conditioning set acting as barriers.

**Algorithm.** To determine which nodes are d-separated from a source set $\mathbf{A}$ given evidence $\mathbf{C}$:

1. Schedule all nodes in $\mathbf{A}$ for visiting (with a marker indicating the ball arrived from a child).
2. Process the schedule. When visiting a node $W$:
   - **If $W \notin \mathbf{C}$ (unobserved) and the ball came from a child:**
     - Pass the ball to all parents of $W$ (the ball flows "upward" through non-observed non-collider).
     - Pass the ball to all children of $W$ (the ball flows "downward" as well).
   - **If $W \notin \mathbf{C}$ (unobserved) and the ball came from a parent:**
     - Pass the ball to all children of $W$ (information flows through the chain/fork).
   - **If $W \in \mathbf{C}$ (observed) and the ball came from a parent:**
     - Do NOT pass the ball to children (the chain/fork is blocked).
     - Pass the ball to all parents of $W$ (diagnostic reasoning — explaining away).
   - **If $W \in \mathbf{C}$ (observed) and the ball came from a child:**
     - Do NOT pass the ball anywhere (completely blocked).
3. The nodes in $\mathbf{B}$ that are not reached by the ball are d-separated from $\mathbf{A}$ given $\mathbf{C}$.

**Complexity:** $O(|\mathbf{V}| + |\mathbf{E}|)$ with careful bookkeeping to avoid revisiting nodes in the same direction.

**Advantages:** The Bayes Ball algorithm is conceptually more transparent than moralization-based methods and maps directly to the intuition of "information flow" through the network. It is the algorithm implemented in many graphical model libraries, including Lutufi.

### 5.5 Worked Example: Social Influence Network

Consider a directed social influence network with the following structure:

```
    SES
   /   \
  v     v
Edu    Net
  \   / |
   v v  v
  Opin  Inc
    |
    v
  Vote
```

Variables:
- **SES** (Socioeconomic Status): root node, no parents
- **Edu** (Education Level): parent: SES
- **Net** (Network Centrality): parent: SES
- **Opin** (Political Opinion): parents: Edu, Net
- **Inc** (Income): parent: Net
- **Vote** (Voting Behavior): parent: Opin

The DAG encodes: $P(\text{SES}, \text{Edu}, \text{Net}, \text{Opin}, \text{Inc}, \text{Vote}) = P(\text{SES}) \cdot P(\text{Edu} \mid \text{SES}) \cdot P(\text{Net} \mid \text{SES}) \cdot P(\text{Opin} \mid \text{Edu}, \text{Net}) \cdot P(\text{Inc} \mid \text{Net}) \cdot P(\text{Vote} \mid \text{Opin})$.

**Query 1:** Is $\text{Edu} \perp_{\mathcal{G}} \text{Inc} \mid \text{SES}$?

Consider paths from Edu to Inc:
- Path 1: $\text{Edu} \leftarrow \text{SES} \to \text{Net} \to \text{Inc}$. SES is a fork and is in the conditioning set $\{SES\}$, so this path is blocked.
- Path 2: $\text{Edu} \to \text{Opin} \leftarrow \text{Net} \to \text{Inc}$. Opin is a collider, and Opin is not in the conditioning set (and Vote, its descendant, is not in the conditioning set either), so the collider blocks this path.

All paths are blocked. **Answer:** $\text{Edu} \perp_{\mathcal{G}} \text{Inc} \mid \text{SES}$. Given socioeconomic status, education and income are independent (income depends only on network centrality, and once SES is known, education provides no additional information about network centrality).

**Query 2:** Is $\text{Edu} \perp_{\mathcal{G}} \text{Inc} \mid \{\text{SES}, \text{Opin}\}$?

- Path 1: $\text{Edu} \leftarrow \text{SES} \to \text{Net} \to \text{Inc}$. SES is a fork and is in the conditioning set, so this path is blocked.
- Path 2: $\text{Edu} \to \text{Opin} \leftarrow \text{Net} \to \text{Inc}$. Opin is a collider. Opin IS in the conditioning set $\{\text{SES}, \text{Opin}\}$, so conditioning on the collider **opens** this path. The path is active.

There is an active path. **Answer:** $\text{Edu} \not\!\perp_{\mathcal{G}} \text{Inc} \mid \{\text{SES}, \text{Opin}\}$. Once we condition on political opinion (a common effect of education and network centrality), education and income become dependent — learning about education tells us something about the component of opinion formation that came from education vs. network centrality, which in turn tells us about network centrality and hence income. This is the explaining-away effect.

**Query 3:** Is $\text{Edu} \perp_{\mathcal{G}} \text{Inc} \mid \{\text{SES}, \text{Vote}\}$?

- Path 2: $\text{Edu} \to \text{Opin} \leftarrow \text{Net} \to \text{Inc}$. Opin is a collider. Opin is not in $\{\text{SES}, \text{Vote}\}$, but Vote is a descendant of Opin and IS in the conditioning set. So the collider is activated by conditioning on a descendant.

**Answer:** $\text{Edu} \not\!\perp_{\mathcal{G}} \text{Inc} \mid \{\text{SES}, \text{Vote}\}$. Conditioning on voting behavior (a downstream consequence of opinion) opens the collider at Opin, creating a spurious dependency between education and income. This illustrates why conditioning on post-treatment variables can introduce bias — a key concern in causal inference.

### 5.6 D-Separation and Conditional Independence: The Formal Connection

**Theorem (Soundness of D-Separation; Verma and Pearl, 1988).** If $P$ is Markov with respect to DAG $\mathcal{G}$ (i.e., $P$ factors according to $\mathcal{G}$), then:

$$
\mathbf{A} \perp_{\mathcal{G}} \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \mathbf{A} \perp\!\!\!\perp \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C} \text{ in } P
$$

D-separation implies conditional independence. This direction always holds.

**Theorem (Completeness of D-Separation; Geiger and Pearl, 1993).** For any DAG $\mathcal{G}$ and any d-connecting path between $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ given $\mathbf{C}$, there exists a distribution $P$ that is Markov with respect to $\mathcal{G}$ in which $\mathbf{A} \not\!\perp\!\!\!\perp \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C}$. That is, d-connection does not necessarily imply dependence in a specific $P$, but it does imply that dependence is *possible*.

The converse — that conditional independence implies d-separation — holds only under the **faithfulness** assumption (see Section 10).

## 6. Separation in Undirected Graphs

### 6.1 The Three Markov Properties for Undirected Graphs

Conditional independence in undirected graphical models (Markov random fields) is governed by three Markov properties, listed from weakest to strongest:

**Pairwise Markov Property.** Two non-adjacent nodes are conditionally independent given all other nodes:

$$
\{i, j\} \notin \mathbf{E} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp X_j \mid \mathbf{X}_{\mathbf{V} \setminus \{i,j\}}
$$

This is the weakest Markov property. It only asserts independence for pairs of non-adjacent nodes, and only when conditioning on *everything else*.

**Local Markov Property.** Each node is conditionally independent of all non-neighbors given its neighbors (Markov blanket):

$$
X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp \mathbf{X}_{\mathbf{V} \setminus (\{i\} \cup \text{Nb}(i))} \mid \mathbf{X}_{\text{Nb}(i)}
$$

where $\text{Nb}(i)$ is the set of neighbors of node $i$. This is stronger than the pairwise property because it asserts independence from all non-neighbors simultaneously, not just individual non-neighbors.

**Global Markov Property.** For any disjoint sets $\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{C}$ such that $\mathbf{C}$ **separates** $\mathbf{A}$ from $\mathbf{B}$ in the graph (i.e., every path from $\mathbf{A}$ to $\mathbf{B}$ passes through $\mathbf{C}$):

$$
\mathbf{A} \perp\!\!\!\perp \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C}
$$

This is the strongest Markov property — it implies both the local and pairwise properties.

### 6.2 Equivalence of Markov Properties

**Theorem (Hammersley-Clifford, 1971; Besag, 1974).** For a probability distribution $P(\mathbf{X}) > 0$ (strictly positive), the following are equivalent:

1. $P$ satisfies the pairwise Markov property with respect to $\mathcal{H}$.
2. $P$ satisfies the local Markov property with respect to $\mathcal{H}$.
3. $P$ satisfies the global Markov property with respect to $\mathcal{H}$.
4. $P$ factorizes over the cliques of $\mathcal{H}$: $P(\mathbf{X}) = \frac{1}{Z} \prod_{C \in \text{Cliques}} \psi_C(\mathbf{X}_C)$.

The strict positivity requirement ($P(\mathbf{X}) > 0$ for all configurations) is essential. Without it, the global property implies the local and pairwise properties, but not vice versa.

**Significance for Lutufi:** This theorem justifies Lutufi's treatment of MRFs. When a social network model uses undirected edges (symmetric relationships), the clique factorization of the distribution is equivalent to the global Markov property — reading separation from the graph gives correct conditional independence statements.

### 6.3 Separation vs. D-Separation

Graph separation in undirected graphs is much simpler than d-separation in DAGs:

**Separation:** $\mathbf{C}$ separates $\mathbf{A}$ from $\mathbf{B}$ in undirected graph $\mathcal{H}$ if every path between $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ passes through at least one node in $\mathbf{C}$.

There are no special cases for colliders, no need to check descendants, no moralization step. The simplicity is both an advantage (easier to compute) and a limitation (fewer CI structures can be represented).

**What undirected graphs cannot represent:** Undirected graphs cannot represent the **explaining away** pattern. If $X$ and $Y$ are marginally independent but conditionally dependent given $Z$ (the collider pattern), no undirected graph over $\{X, Y, Z\}$ can encode this. Any undirected graph that makes $X$ and $Y$ marginally independent (no edge between them) will also make them conditionally independent given $Z$.

**What DAGs cannot represent:** DAGs cannot directly represent certain symmetric independence patterns. For example, in a four-node cycle $X_1 - X_2 - X_3 - X_4 - X_1$, the undirected graph encodes $X_1 \perp\!\!\!\perp X_3 \mid \{X_2, X_4\}$ and $X_2 \perp\!\!\!\perp X_4 \mid \{X_1, X_3\}$. No DAG can encode both of these simultaneously.

### 6.4 Factor Graph Separation

In Lutufi's factor graph representation, separation combines aspects of both DAG and undirected separation. Two variable nodes $X$ and $Y$ are separated by a set $\mathbf{C}$ of variable nodes in the factor graph if every path from $X$ to $Y$ that alternates between variable nodes and factor nodes passes through some variable node in $\mathbf{C}$.

This is a natural generalization that handles both directed and undirected CI structures when the factor graph is derived from the appropriate model type.

## 7. The Markov Blanket

### 7.1 Definition

The **Markov blanket** of a variable $X_i$, denoted $\text{MB}(X_i)$, is the minimal set of variables that renders $X_i$ conditionally independent of all other variables:

$$
X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp \mathbf{X}_{\mathbf{V} \setminus (\{i\} \cup \text{MB}(X_i))} \mid \mathbf{X}_{\text{MB}(X_i)}
$$

The Markov blanket is the "informational boundary" of $X_i$ — beyond it, no additional variable provides information about $X_i$.

### 7.2 Markov Blanket in Different Graph Types

**In a Bayesian network (DAG):** The Markov blanket of $X_i$ consists of:
1. **Parents of $X_i$:** $\text{Pa}(X_i)$
2. **Children of $X_i$:** $\text{Ch}(X_i)$
3. **Co-parents** (other parents of $X_i$'s children): $\bigcup_{Y \in \text{Ch}(X_i)} \text{Pa}(Y) \setminus \{X_i\}$

The co-parents are included because of the explaining-away effect: conditioning on a child of $X_i$ opens the collider path between $X_i$ and the child's other parents.

**In an undirected graph (MRF):** The Markov blanket of $X_i$ is simply the **neighborhood** of $X_i$ — the set of nodes directly connected to $X_i$ by an edge:

$$
\text{MB}(X_i) = \text{Nb}(X_i)
$$

This simplicity is a consequence of the fact that undirected graphs have no colliders and hence no explaining-away effects.

**In a factor graph:** The Markov blanket consists of all variable nodes that share at least one factor node with $X_i$.

### 7.3 Computing the Markov Blanket

**Algorithm for DAGs:**
1. Initialize $\text{MB}(X_i) = \emptyset$.
2. Add all parents of $X_i$.
3. Add all children of $X_i$.
4. For each child $Y$ of $X_i$, add all parents of $Y$ except $X_i$ itself.

**Complexity:** $O(\text{degree of } X_i + \sum_{Y \in \text{Ch}(X_i)} \text{in-degree of } Y)$, which is $O(|\mathbf{E}|)$ in the worst case but typically much smaller.

### 7.4 Significance for Inference

**Gibbs sampling.** The full conditional distribution of $X_i$ given all other variables depends only on the Markov blanket:

$$
P(X_i \mid \mathbf{X}_{\mathbf{V} \setminus \{i\}}) = P(X_i \mid \mathbf{X}_{\text{MB}(X_i)})
$$

For Bayesian networks, this has a simple closed form:

$$
P(X_i \mid \mathbf{X}_{\text{MB}(X_i)}) \propto P(X_i \mid \text{Pa}(X_i)) \prod_{Y \in \text{Ch}(X_i)} P(Y \mid \text{Pa}(Y))
$$

This is the product of $X_i$'s own CPT entry and the CPT entries of all its children, evaluated at their current values. This formula makes Gibbs sampling in Bayesian networks efficient: sampling each variable requires computing a product over only its Markov blanket members.

**Feature selection.** In a predictive model where $X_i$ is the target variable, the Markov blanket provides the optimal feature set — the minimal set of variables that contains all relevant information for predicting $X_i$. This follows directly from the definition: given the Markov blanket, no other variable can improve prediction because they are all conditionally independent of $X_i$.

The **Grow-Shrink (GS) algorithm** (Margaritis and Thrun, 2000) and **IAMB (Incremental Association Markov Blanket)** algorithm (Tsamardinos et al., 2003) exploit this property for feature selection using CI tests.

### 7.5 Markov Blanket in Social Networks

In a social network context, the Markov blanket of a person represents their immediate "informational environment" — the set of people whose states are sufficient to predict the person's behavior. This concept has deep connections to sociology:

**Strong ties and weak ties.** Granovetter's (1973) theory of weak ties suggests that information often flows through weak, peripheral connections rather than strong, central ones. The Markov blanket analysis can identify whether weak ties carry information beyond what strong ties (parents and children in the DAG) already provide — if a weak tie is not in the Markov blanket, it is informationally redundant.

**Structural holes.** Burt's (1992) theory of structural holes identifies valuable network positions as those bridging otherwise disconnected groups. The Markov blanket of a node spanning a structural hole will include members from both sides of the bridge — reflecting the node's role as an information conduit.

**Echo chambers.** If a person's Markov blanket consists entirely of like-minded individuals, the person is in an informational echo chamber — their belief state is determined entirely by their local, homogeneous social context. Lutufi can compute Markov blanket homogeneity as a diagnostic for echo chamber effects.

## 8. Testing Conditional Independence from Data

In practice, conditional independence relationships are not given; they must be inferred from observed data. Testing conditional independence is a core operation in constraint-based structure learning (PC algorithm, FCI algorithm) and model validation. This section surveys the statistical methods for CI testing and their challenges.

### 8.1 The Hypothesis Testing Framework

The standard approach formulates CI testing as a hypothesis test:

- **Null hypothesis $H_0$:** $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$ (conditional independence holds).
- **Alternative hypothesis $H_1$:** $X \not\!\perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$ (conditional dependence).

A test statistic is computed from data, and $H_0$ is rejected if the statistic exceeds a threshold determined by the desired significance level $\alpha$.

**Fundamental asymmetry:** Hypothesis testing can reject independence (find evidence of dependence) but cannot positively confirm it. Failure to reject $H_0$ does not prove independence — it means there is insufficient evidence of dependence. This asymmetry is a fundamental limitation of constraint-based structure learning.

### 8.2 Chi-Squared Test

For discrete variables, the Pearson **chi-squared test** for CI compares observed and expected frequencies under the independence assumption.

For discrete variables $X$ (with $r$ categories), $Y$ (with $c$ categories), and $Z$ (with $q$ categories), the conditional chi-squared statistic is:

$$
\chi^2 = \sum_{z=1}^{q} \sum_{x=1}^{r} \sum_{y=1}^{c} \frac{(N_{xyz} - E_{xyz})^2}{E_{xyz}}
$$

where $N_{xyz}$ is the observed count of $(X=x, Y=y, Z=z)$ and $E_{xyz} = \frac{N_{x \cdot z} \cdot N_{\cdot y z}}{N_{\cdot \cdot z}}$ is the expected count under $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$.

Under $H_0$, this statistic follows a $\chi^2$ distribution with $q \cdot (r-1)(c-1)$ degrees of freedom (asymptotically, as $N \to \infty$).

**Limitations:**
- Requires sufficient sample size in each cell ($N_{xyz} \geq 5$ is a common rule of thumb).
- As the conditioning set $Z$ grows, the number of cells grows exponentially, making the test unreliable due to sparse data.
- Does not detect nonlinear dependencies between continuous variables.

### 8.3 G-Test (Likelihood Ratio Test)

The **G-test** (or log-likelihood ratio test) is an alternative to the chi-squared test with better theoretical properties:

$$
G = 2 \sum_{z} \sum_{x} \sum_{y} N_{xyz} \log \frac{N_{xyz} \cdot N_{\cdot \cdot z}}{N_{x \cdot z} \cdot N_{\cdot y z}}
$$

Under $H_0$, $G$ asymptotically follows the same $\chi^2$ distribution as the Pearson statistic. The G-test is preferred in information-theoretic analyses because $G = 2N \cdot \hat{I}(X; Y \mid Z)$, where $\hat{I}$ is the empirical conditional mutual information.

**Advantages over chi-squared:**
- More naturally connected to information-theoretic quantities.
- Additively decomposable across subsets of the data.
- Better asymptotic properties in certain regimes.

### 8.4 Mutual Information Tests

The **conditional mutual information** (CMI) directly quantifies the strength of dependence:

$$
\hat{I}(X; Y \mid Z) = \sum_{z} P(z) \sum_{x} \sum_{y} P(x, y \mid z) \log \frac{P(x, y \mid z)}{P(x \mid z) P(y \mid z)}
$$

$\hat{I}(X; Y \mid Z) = 0$ if and only if $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$. The empirical version uses frequency-based probability estimates.

**Permutation-based testing:** Rather than relying on asymptotic distributions, one can assess significance by permutation:

1. Compute $\hat{I}(X; Y \mid Z)$ on the observed data.
2. Repeatedly permute the values of $Y$ within each stratum defined by $Z$, destroying any association while preserving $P(Y \mid Z)$.
3. Compute $\hat{I}$ on each permuted dataset to build a null distribution.
4. Compare the observed $\hat{I}$ to the null distribution.

This approach is valid for any sample size but computationally expensive.

### 8.5 Partial Correlation Tests

For continuous variables, the **partial correlation** between $X$ and $Y$ given $Z$ measures linear conditional dependence:

$$
\rho_{XY \cdot Z} = \frac{\rho_{XY} - \rho_{XZ} \rho_{YZ}}{\sqrt{(1 - \rho_{XZ}^2)(1 - \rho_{YZ}^2)}}
$$

For multivariate $Z = (Z_1, \ldots, Z_k)$, the partial correlation is computed by recursive application of this formula, or equivalently by regressing $X$ and $Y$ on $Z$ and computing the correlation of the residuals.

Under the assumption that $(X, Y, Z)$ is jointly Gaussian, $\rho_{XY \cdot Z} = 0 \Leftrightarrow X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$. The test statistic:

$$
t = \rho_{XY \cdot Z} \sqrt{\frac{N - |Z| - 2}{1 - \rho_{XY \cdot Z}^2}}
$$

follows a Student's $t$-distribution with $N - |Z| - 2$ degrees of freedom under $H_0$.

**Limitations:**
- Only detects linear associations. If $X$ and $Y$ are related nonlinearly (e.g., $Y = X^2$), the partial correlation may be zero despite strong dependence.
- Assumes joint Gaussianity for the validity of the $t$-test.
- Undefined when $|Z| \geq N - 2$ (more conditioning variables than data points minus 2).

### 8.6 Kernel-Based Tests (HSIC and KCI)

For general (possibly nonlinear, non-Gaussian) dependencies, **kernel-based independence tests** provide a powerful nonparametric alternative.

**Hilbert-Schmidt Independence Criterion (HSIC)** (Gretton et al., 2005):

HSIC measures the dependence between two variables by embedding their joint distribution into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) and measuring the distance between the joint embedding and the product of the marginal embeddings:

$$
\text{HSIC}(X, Y) = \| \text{Cov}_{XY} \|_{\text{HS}}^2
$$

where $\text{Cov}_{XY}$ is the cross-covariance operator in the RKHS and $\| \cdot \|_{\text{HS}}$ is the Hilbert-Schmidt norm.

**Kernel Conditional Independence Test (KCI)** (Zhang et al., 2011):

KCI extends HSIC to conditional independence testing. It regresses the kernel matrices of $X$ and $Y$ on $Z$ and tests whether the residual kernel matrices are independent.

**Advantages:**
- Detects arbitrary nonlinear dependencies.
- Makes minimal assumptions about the distribution.
- Consistent — with enough data, it correctly identifies all dependencies.

**Limitations:**
- Computationally expensive: $O(N^3)$ for the standard implementation due to kernel matrix eigendecomposition.
- Choice of kernel and bandwidth affects power.
- Difficult to interpret the "type" of dependence detected.

### 8.7 Challenges with Finite Samples

All CI tests face fundamental challenges with finite data:

**Multiple testing.** Structure learning algorithms (e.g., the PC algorithm) perform many CI tests — potentially $O(n^2 \cdot 2^n)$ in the worst case. Without correction for multiple testing, the probability of false discoveries (Type I errors) becomes very high. Common corrections include:
- **Bonferroni correction:** Divide $\alpha$ by the number of tests. Conservative.
- **Benjamini-Hochberg (BH) procedure:** Controls the false discovery rate (FDR). Less conservative.
- **Permutation-based corrections:** Control the family-wise error rate without parametric assumptions.

**Conditioning set size.** As the conditioning set $Z$ grows, the data is split into more and more strata, reducing the effective sample size per stratum. For discrete variables with $k$ states each and a conditioning set of size $d$, the number of strata is $k^d$, and the average sample size per stratum is $N / k^d$. When this drops below approximately 5–10, tests become unreliable.

**Curse of dimensionality.** For continuous conditioning variables, the data in any local region of the conditioning space becomes sparse as the dimensionality of $Z$ increases. This is the statistical curse of dimensionality — the data required for reliable testing grows exponentially with the conditioning set size.

**Near-independence.** Many real-world conditional dependencies are weak but non-zero. Detecting them requires large samples, but distinguishing them from exact independence is often practically irrelevant. Some algorithms use a minimum dependence threshold rather than a strict hypothesis test.

### 8.8 Bayesian Approaches to CI Testing

An alternative to frequentist hypothesis testing is the **Bayesian approach**, which computes the posterior probability that the CI holds. Margaritis (2005) proposed:

$$
P(X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z \mid \text{Data}) = \frac{P(\text{Data} \mid X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z) \cdot P(X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z)}{P(\text{Data})}
$$

The ratio $\frac{P(\text{Data} \mid X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z)}{P(\text{Data} \mid X \not\!\perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z)}$ is a **Bayes factor** that directly quantifies the evidence for or against independence. The Bayesian approach has the advantage of providing graded evidence rather than a binary accept/reject decision, and it naturally incorporates prior information about the expected sparsity of the graph.

## 9. Conditional Independence in Social Networks

Social networks present unique challenges and opportunities for conditional independence. The concepts from Sections 2–8 were developed primarily in the context of statistical models and machine learning; applying them to social and economic networks requires careful consideration of what CI means in a social context, when it holds, and when it systematically fails.

### 9.1 What Conditional Independence Means Socially

In a social network, conditional independence $X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp X_j \mid \mathbf{X}_C$ means: given the states (beliefs, behaviors, outcomes) of the people in set $C$, knowing person $i$'s state provides no additional information about person $j$'s state. The people in $C$ fully mediate or explain any association between $i$ and $j$.

**Informational interpretation.** Suppose $C$ lies on all paths between $i$ and $j$ in the social graph. CI says that all information flowing from $i$ to $j$ (or shared between them) passes through $C$. Once $C$ is known, $i$ and $j$ are informationally disconnected.

**Causal interpretation.** If the graph is causal, $X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp X_j \mid \mathbf{X}_C$ means that there is no causal pathway from $i$ to $j$ (or from $j$ to $i$) that bypasses $C$. Intervening on $i$'s state would not change $j$'s state once $C$ is fixed.

### 9.2 When Conditional Independence Holds in Social Networks

CI tends to hold in social networks when:

**The network is sparse.** In sparse networks (average degree $\bar{k} \ll n$), most pairs of nodes are far apart, and d-separation (or graph separation) is common. The conditioning sets needed to separate nodes are small (of the order of the graph's expansion rate).

**Influence is local.** When influence decays rapidly with social distance — each person primarily affects their direct contacts, with negligible second-order effects — the local Markov property holds well. This is the regime where graphical model assumptions are most appropriate.

**Community structure is strong.** When the network has clear community structure (high modularity), inter-community CI is often achievable by conditioning on the few "bridge" nodes between communities. Lutufi exploits this by identifying community structure and using it to guide inference.

### 9.3 When Conditional Independence Fails in Social Networks

CI systematically fails in social networks in several important scenarios:

**Latent confounders.** Two people may appear conditionally dependent even after conditioning on all observed variables, because they share an unobserved common cause. In social networks, latent confounders are ubiquitous: shared cultural background, common exposure to media, correlated personality traits that influence both network formation and behavior.

**Example.** Suppose individuals $A$ and $B$ both adopt a new technology. Their adoption times are correlated even after controlling for their common network neighbors. This correlation might be due to a latent common cause — perhaps both $A$ and $B$ are "early adopters" by temperament, a trait not captured in the observed variables. The CI $A \perp\!\!\!\perp B \mid \text{neighbors}$ fails because of the latent confounder.

**Homophily.** Homophily — the tendency for similar people to form ties — creates correlations that look like influence but are actually selection effects. If high-income people preferentially befriend other high-income people, then conditioning on network position (which high-income people share) does not remove the income correlation — because the correlation was there before the network formed.

**Formally:** Let $X_i$ and $X_j$ be the behaviors of $i$ and $j$, let $N_{ij}$ be their network tie, and let $H$ be a homophily variable (shared trait). The causal structure is often:

$$
H \to X_i, \quad H \to X_j, \quad H \to N_{ij}
$$

Here, $X_i$ and $X_j$ are d-separated by $H$, but $H$ is unobserved. Conditioning on $N_{ij}$ (a descendant of $H$) opens the path through the collider, creating the spurious dependence. This is a specific instance of collider bias in the social context.

**Network interference.** In settings where one person's treatment affects another's outcome (violation of SUTVA — the Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption), conditional independence in the standard sense does not hold. If vaccinating person $A$ reduces person $B$'s disease risk through herd immunity, then $B$'s outcome is not independent of $A$'s treatment even after conditioning on $B$'s own treatment and their shared environment.

**Reciprocal causation and cycles.** Social influence is often reciprocal — person $A$ influences person $B$, who in turn influences $A$. This creates cycles in the dependency graph, violating the DAG assumption. In cyclic systems, d-separation is not defined in the standard sense, and the conditional independence structure may be different from what any DAG would predict.

**Contagion processes.** In social contagion (spread of behaviors, diseases, or information), the dependencies propagate through the entire connected component. Every node is potentially dependent on every other node, with the dependencies attenuating but never reaching zero with social distance. The local Markov property — that conditioning on a node's neighbors screens it off from the rest of the network — may fail because contagion creates long-range dependencies.

### 9.4 The Manski Reflection Problem

Charles Manski (1993) identified a fundamental challenge in separating social influence from correlated effects, known as the **reflection problem.** Consider a group where each member's outcome depends on the group's average outcome:

$$
Y_i = \alpha + \beta \cdot \bar{Y}_{-i} + \gamma \cdot Z_i + \epsilon_i
$$

where $\bar{Y}_{-i}$ is the average outcome in $i$'s peer group (excluding $i$), and $Z_i$ is an individual characteristic. The coefficient $\beta$ represents "endogenous social effects" (the group's outcome affects the individual).

Manski showed that $\beta$ is not identifiable from observational data without additional assumptions, because the equation is reflective: $Y_i$ affects $\bar{Y}_{-i}$ just as $\bar{Y}_{-i}$ affects $Y_i$. This is a fundamental CI violation — the variables cannot be separated because they are simultaneous.

For Lutufi, this means that CI-based structure learning cannot, in general, distinguish social influence from correlated effects in cross-sectional network data. Temporal (panel) data or exogenous shocks are needed to identify the direction and existence of causal effects.

## 10. Faithfulness and Its Violations

### 10.1 Formal Definition

Let $\mathcal{G}$ be a DAG and $P$ be a probability distribution. $P$ is **Markov** with respect to $\mathcal{G}$ if every d-separation in $\mathcal{G}$ implies a conditional independence in $P$:

$$
\mathbf{A} \perp_{\mathcal{G}} \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \mathbf{A} \perp\!\!\!\perp \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C} \text{ in } P
$$

$P$ is **faithful** to $\mathcal{G}$ if the converse also holds:

$$
\mathbf{A} \perp\!\!\!\perp \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C} \text{ in } P \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \mathbf{A} \perp_{\mathcal{G}} \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C}
$$

Equivalently, $P$ is faithful to $\mathcal{G}$ if the conditional independence relations in $P$ are **exactly** those implied by d-separation in $\mathcal{G}$ — no more, no less.

**Combining the Markov and faithfulness conditions:** $P$ is faithful to $\mathcal{G}$ if and only if:

$$
\mathbf{A} \perp\!\!\!\perp \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C} \text{ in } P \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \mathbf{A} \perp_{\mathcal{G}} \mathbf{B} \mid \mathbf{C}
$$

This biconditional is the strongest link between graph structure and probabilistic independence. It allows us to read off all CI relationships from the graph and, conversely, to learn the graph from CI relationships.

### 10.2 Why Faithfulness Matters

Faithfulness is critical for:

**Structure learning.** Constraint-based algorithms (PC, FCI, IC) discover graph structure by testing CI relationships in data and then finding a graph consistent with those relationships. If faithfulness fails — if there are CI relationships in the data not implied by any graph — these algorithms will learn an incorrect structure.

**Model interpretation.** If a model is faithful, every active path in the graph corresponds to a real dependence in the data. This makes the graph structure directly interpretable as a map of actual statistical relationships. Without faithfulness, some edges may be "dead" — present in the graph but corresponding to zero-strength dependencies.

**Causal identification.** Do-calculus and the ID algorithm rely on faithfulness (or variants like "d-separation faithfulness") for identifying causal effects from observational data.

### 10.3 What Unfaithful Distributions Look Like

Unfaithfulness occurs when the parameters of a model conspire to create conditional independencies that the graph structure does not predict. The classic example involves **path cancellation.**

**Example: Perfect cancellation.** Consider a DAG with three variables: $X \to Y$ and $X \to Z \to Y$. The graph has two paths from $X$ to $Y$ — a direct path and an indirect path through $Z$. D-separation says that $X \not\!\perp_{\mathcal{G}} Y$ (unconditionally), because there is an active path.

But suppose the direct effect of $X$ on $Y$ is $+\beta$ and the indirect effect (through $Z$) is exactly $-\beta$. Then the total effect of $X$ on $Y$ is $\beta + (-\beta) = 0$, and $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y$ in the distribution — despite the graph having an active path. This is an unfaithful distribution.

**In the linear Gaussian case:** Consider the structural equations:
- $X \sim N(0, 1)$
- $Z = aX + \epsilon_Z$
- $Y = bX + cZ + \epsilon_Y$

The marginal correlation between $X$ and $Y$ is $\text{Corr}(X, Y) = b + ac$ (ignoring normalizations). If $b + ac = 0$ (i.e., $b = -ac$), then $X$ and $Y$ are marginally uncorrelated (and, for Gaussians, unconditionally independent). The distribution satisfies $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y$ even though the graph has $X$ and $Y$ connected by active paths. This is unfaithfulness due to exact cancellation.

### 10.4 How Common Are Faithfulness Violations?

**Measure-zero result.** Meek (1995) and Spirtes et al. (2000) showed that the set of unfaithful parameterizations has **Lebesgue measure zero** in the parameter space. This means that if parameters are drawn from any continuous distribution (or even any distribution with a density), the probability of exact unfaithfulness is zero.

**Near-unfaithfulness.** While exact unfaithfulness is measure-zero, **near-unfaithfulness** — where the CI relationship approximately holds to within the resolution of any finite-sample test — is not measure-zero and can be practically common. Uhler et al. (2013) showed that the set of distributions that are "nearly unfaithful" (where some dependence is weaker than any statistical test can detect at a given sample size) can have substantial measure, especially for:
- Dense graphs with many paths between nodes
- Models with opposing positive and negative effects
- Models with many mediating variables

**In social networks:** Near-unfaithfulness is a genuine concern. Social influence networks often have both positive (reinforcing) and negative (opposing) pathways — for example, a person may receive both pro and con arguments from different neighbors. If these effects partially cancel, the observed dependence may be much weaker than individual pathway effects, leading to spurious independence in finite samples.

### 10.5 Implications for Structure Learning

When faithfulness fails or approximately fails:

- **The PC algorithm** may add spurious edges (failing to remove an edge because the test did not find independence, when faithfulness violation makes the true independence undetectable).
- **Score-based methods** (BIC, BDeu, GES) are generally more robust to faithfulness violations because they optimize a global fit criterion rather than relying on individual CI tests.
- **The SGS (Spirtes-Glymour-Scheines) theorem** states that if the true distribution is faithful to some DAG, then the PC algorithm will recover the correct Markov equivalence class in the large-sample limit. If faithfulness fails, no such guarantee exists.

**Lutufi's approach:** Lutufi implements both constraint-based and score-based structure learning. When constraint-based methods are used, Lutufi provides diagnostics for potential faithfulness violations:
1. **Near-zero partial correlations** along edges: suggests the edge may be an artifact of near-unfaithfulness.
2. **Conflicting CI test results** at different significance levels: suggests that the dependence is near the detection threshold.
3. **Multiple competing path effects**: identifies graph structures (multiple directed paths between nodes) where cancellation is possible.

### 10.6 Alternatives to Faithfulness

Several weaker assumptions have been proposed:

**Adjacency faithfulness** (Ramsey et al., 2006): If $X$ and $Y$ are adjacent in the true graph, then they are dependent conditional on every subset of the remaining variables. This is weaker than full faithfulness (it does not constrain non-adjacent pairs) and rules out the most problematic violations.

**Orientation faithfulness** (Ramsey et al., 2006): If $X \to Z \leftarrow Y$ is a v-structure (collider) in the true graph, then $X$ and $Y$ are dependent conditional on any set containing $Z$ (or $Z$'s descendants) but not containing any other separator. This ensures that collider orientations are identifiable.

**SGS-minimality** (Spirtes et al., 2000): The graph is the simplest (fewest edges) among all graphs whose d-separations are all true in $P$. This is implied by faithfulness but does not require the converse.

## 11. How Lutufi Implements Conditional Independence

### 11.1 CI Queries

Lutufi provides a first-class interface for conditional independence queries. Given a model (Bayesian network, MRF, or factor graph), users can ask:

**Graph-based CI queries:**
- "Are nodes $A$ and $B$ d-separated by $C$ in the graph?" — This is answered by the Bayes Ball algorithm (for DAGs) or graph separation (for undirected graphs), with no reference to the actual parameters or data.

- "What is the Markov blanket of node $X$?" — Computed from the graph structure alone.

- "What are all CI relationships implied by this graph?" — Enumerates the set of conditional independencies entailed by d-separation (or separation). This is computationally expensive for large graphs but invaluable for model auditing.

**Distribution-based CI queries:**
- "What is the conditional mutual information $I(X; Y \mid Z)$ under the model?" — Computed from the parametric model using inference. If $I(X; Y \mid Z) = 0$ (to within numerical tolerance), the CI holds in the model.

- "Does the model satisfy faithfulness?" — Checks whether every CI in the distribution corresponds to a d-separation in the graph. Flags potential unfaithfulness.

### 11.2 CI Testing from Data

Lutufi implements the following CI tests for use in structure learning and model validation:

| Test | Variable Type | Detects | Complexity |
|------|--------------|---------|------------|
| Chi-squared | Discrete | Linear and nonlinear | $O(N \cdot r \cdot c \cdot q)$ |
| G-test | Discrete | Linear and nonlinear | $O(N \cdot r \cdot c \cdot q)$ |
| Partial correlation + Fisher's z | Continuous (Gaussian) | Linear | $O(N \cdot d)$ |
| KCI (Kernel CI) | Continuous (any) | Nonlinear | $O(N^3)$ |
| CMI permutation | Discrete or continuous | Any | $O(N \cdot B)$ (B = permutation count) |

The default test selection is automatic:
- Discrete variables → G-test (with chi-squared as a fallback for very small samples).
- Continuous Gaussian variables → Partial correlation test.
- Continuous non-Gaussian or mixed → KCI (when computationally feasible) or CMI permutation test.

### 11.3 CI-Based Structure Learning

Lutufi's implementation of the PC algorithm uses CI testing as its core subroutine:

**Phase 1 — Skeleton Discovery.**
1. Start with a complete undirected graph over all variables.
2. For each pair $(X, Y)$, test $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid \mathbf{S}$ for conditioning sets $\mathbf{S}$ of increasing size, drawn from the current neighbors of $X$ and $Y$.
3. If $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid \mathbf{S}$ for some $\mathbf{S}$, remove the edge between $X$ and $Y$ and record $\mathbf{S}$ as the separating set.
4. Stop increasing the conditioning set size when it exceeds the current degree minus 1 for each node.

**Phase 2 — V-Structure Identification.**
For each triple $X - Z - Y$ (where $X$ and $Y$ are non-adjacent but both adjacent to $Z$):
- If $Z$ is NOT in the separating set of $X$ and $Y$, orient the edges as $X \to Z \leftarrow Y$ (v-structure).

**Phase 3 — Orientation Propagation.**
Apply Meek's rules (Meek, 1995) to orient additional edges based on the constraint that no new v-structures or cycles are created.

**Output:** A CPDAG (Completed Partially Directed Acyclic Graph) representing the Markov equivalence class of the true DAG (under faithfulness and the Markov assumption).

**Scalability.** The order of the conditioning set determines the computational cost. Lutufi uses the **order-independent** variant of PC (PC-stable; Colombo and Maathuis, 2014) which avoids the issue of results depending on the order in which pairs are processed. For large graphs, Lutufi employs parallelized CI testing, testing multiple pairs and conditioning sets simultaneously.

### 11.4 User-Facing API Concepts

Lutufi exposes CI as a core concept through its API with the following key operations:

- **`model.is_independent(A, B, given=C)`** — Tests whether nodes $A$ and $B$ are d-separated (or separated) by $C$ in the graph.

- **`model.markov_blanket(X)`** — Returns the Markov blanket of node $X$.

- **`model.all_independencies()`** — Returns all CI relations implied by the graph structure.

- **`data.test_independence(X, Y, Z, method='auto')`** — Performs a statistical CI test on the dataset.

- **`model.check_faithfulness(data)`** — Compares graph-implied CI with data-estimated CI, flagging discrepancies.

- **`model.learn_structure(data, method='pc')`** — Learns the graph structure from data using CI-based (or score-based) methods.

These abstractions allow domain scientists — sociologists, economists, epidemiologists — to reason about conditional independence without needing to manually implement the underlying algorithms. Lutufi handles the translation between the conceptual question ("is $X$ independent of $Y$ controlling for $Z$?") and the computational procedure (d-separation, CI tests, structure learning).
